Booking app for a cafe

Design a booking app for a cafe

The Product

JavaJunction is a caffe based in small town near Seattle, WA. Javajunction wants to cater to people who want easy option to order coffee online who are busy during business/office hours and have no time to stand in cue. Easy option for pickup during their commute or get their order via delivery. Split bill option so that they can easy maintain their record and when have no time to ask for money.


Project Duration

February'23 to April'23

Overview

The Problem:

Busy working people often skip their coffee and meal during their tight schedule in business hours. Some of them have issue in maintaining records to track of money they have ordered for larger groups.

The Goal:

Design an app for JavaJunction that allow user to easy order their coffee and get their bill split easily if ordered for larger group.

My Role:

UX designer designing an app for Javajunction for conception of pickup and delivery and split bill option.

The Responsibilty:

Conducting interviews, paper and digital wireframing, low and high-fidelity prototyping, conducting usability studies, accounting for accessibility, and iterating on designs.

Understanding the user

User Research

I conducted interviews and created empathy maps to understand the users. I’m designing for and their needs. A primary user group identified through research was working adults who don’t have time to go to café to stand in cue for ordering. This user group confirmed initial assumptions about JavaJunction customers, but research also revealed that time was one of the main factor which was limiting users from having coffee. Other user problems included interests in coffee or tea, or it becomes cool on the way to their office and challenge for them to ask money from there colleagues or friends with whom they have ordered.

Pain Points

1

Time: Working adults are too busy to stand in cue for order.

2

Accessibility: Platforms for ordering food are not equipped with assistive technologies or don’t have delivery options.

3

Payment: Payment for larger groups become difficult.

Starting the Design
Paper Wireframe

Taking the time to draft iterations of each screen of the app on paper ensured that the elements that made it to digital wireframes would be well-suited to address user pain points. For the home screen, I prioritized a quick and easy ordering process to help users save time.

*Stars were used to mark the elements of each sketch that would be used in the initial digital wireframes.

Digital Wireframe

*As the initial design phase continued, I made sure to base screen designs on feedback and findings from the user research.

Low-Fidelity Prototype

Using the completed set of digital wireframes, I created a low-fidelity prototype. The primary user flow I connected was building and ordering a drink, so the prototype could be used in a usability study. View the JavaJunction App low-fidelity prototype

Usability Study-Findings

I conducted two rounds of usability studies. Findings from the first study helped guide the designs from wireframes to mockups. The second study used a high-fidelity prototype and revealed what aspects of the mockups needed refining.

Round-1 Findings

  • Users need a way to easy navigate between tab and different screens.
  • Users need separate customization options while ordering coffee.
  • Users need a option to pay online and split with friends options while ordering online.

Round-2 Findings

  • Customisation screen didn’t had add to cart visible or made user feel that they have to swipe up.
  • “Split your bill” was not accessible.
Redefining the Designs
Mockups

Early designs didn’t made user feel that they have to swipe up for adding the product in the cart, so in the hi-fi prototype I have made changes so that add to cart is slightly visible and have made user feel that they have to swipe up.

Accessibility

Accessibility considerations

    • Provided access to users who are vision impaired through adding alt text to images for screen readers.
    • Used icons to help make navigation easier.
    • Used detailed imagery of the product help all users better understand the designs.

Going Forward

Takeaways

Impact:

The app makes users feel like JavaJunction really thinks about how to meet their needs.

One quote from peer feedback: “The app will really help to have my drinks ordered in advance and have them pickup and pickmyself on my free time.”

What I learned:

While designing the JavaJunction app, I learned that the first ideas for the app are only the beginning of the process. Usability studies and peer feedback influenced each iteration of the app’s designs.

Next Steps

1

Conduct another round of usability studies to validate whether the pain points users experienced have been effectively addressed.

2

Conduct more user research to determine any new areas of need.
